# Competitive Landscape

## Major Players (2026 Status)

### Angi (formerly HomeAdvisor + Angie's List)

Angi is the cautionary tale of the home services industry. After the 2017 merger of HomeAdvisor and Angie's List, the company operated under IAC until spinning off as an independent public company in 2023. The results since independence have been brutal.

**Financial trajectory:**
- Peak revenue: ~$1.8B (2022). Current run rate: ~$1.2B. That is a 30%+ decline in under four years.
- Stock performance post-IAC split has been dismal. The company trades at a fraction of its 2021 highs, with market cap hovering around $2B compared to $12B+ at peak.
- Free cash flow has deteriorated as customer acquisition costs rise and contractor retention drops.
- The company has cycled through strategic pivots — fixed-price services, pre-priced projects, subscription bundles — none of which have reversed the decline.

**Contractor cost structure:**
- Hybrid model: $0-$350/mo subscription fee (varies by trade, market, and tier) PLUS per-lead charges on top.
- Average lead cost: ~$135, but this varies dramatically by trade. Plumbing leads can run $25-60. Roofing leads run $80-150. Kitchen remodel or addition leads run $200-350. HVAC leads during summer peak can hit $175+.
- Each lead is sent to 2-4 competing contractors simultaneously. Win rate on shared leads is roughly 25-30%, meaning effective cost per acquired customer is $400-540 for an average trade.
- Contractors report spending $1,500-$3,000/month on Angi with highly variable ROI.

**Contractor sentiment:**
- Angi has the most negative contractor sentiment of any platform in the industry. This is well-documented across contractor forums, Reddit, and industry publications.
- Common complaints: subscription fees continue even when leads are scarce, difficulty canceling (contractors report multi-step cancellation processes with retention calls), declining lead quality over time, leads that are clearly shopping price not quality, and duplicate leads across the Angi/HomeAdvisor legacy systems.
- A recurring theme on contractor forums: "I spent $2,400 last month on Angi and got one job worth $800."
- The trust deficit is severe. Angi's brand among contractors is damaged in ways that are difficult to repair.

**Strategic implication for FTW:** Angi's decline creates a vacuum. There are hundreds of thousands of contractors who have been burned by the lead-gen model and are looking for alternatives. FTW's zero-lead-fee model is a direct answer to their biggest pain point.

### Thumbtack

- Revenue: $400M+ from marketplace fees, $1B+ total annually. Up 33% YoY.
- The strongest and most dangerous competitor. See [thumbtack-deep-dive.md](thumbtack-deep-dive.md) for full analysis.
- Partnerships with ChatGPT, Amazon Alexa, Zillow, Redfin give them distribution advantages no other player has.
- Growing aggressively while Angi shrinks. They are consolidating the lead-gen market.
- Their weakness: generalist platform (DJs, tutors, dog walkers alongside contractors), no construction AI, no QuickBooks integration, no construction-specific depth.

### Houzz Pro

Houzz pivoted away from lead generation as a primary business and rebuilt around Houzz Pro, a construction management SaaS tool aimed at residential remodelers and design-build firms.

**Feature comparison with FTW:**

| Feature | Houzz Pro | FTW |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| Estimation | Manual takeoffs, templates | AI-powered (ConstructionAI) |
| Lead generation | Secondary add-on, design-focused | Core marketplace with matching |
| Payments | In-platform processing | QuickBooks-native (direct to contractor) |
| Project management | Full suite (scheduling, selections, daily logs) | Core features in free tier |
| Pricing | $85-$399/mo depending on tier | $0-$149/mo, zero lead fees |
| Target market | Design-build firms, remodelers | All residential contractors |
| AI capabilities | None specific to estimation | Fine-tuned Llama 3.1 8B model |
| Marketplace | Design inspiration + contractor profiles | Two-sided bidding marketplace |

**Key difference:** Houzz Pro is a solid SaaS tool but it lacks AI estimation and a true two-sided marketplace. Their lead flow is derivative of their design inspiration platform (homeowners browse photos, find contractors), not a purpose-built matching system. Their user base skews toward design-focused remodelers, not general contractors, framers, roofers, or concrete crews.

**Strategic implication for FTW:** Houzz Pro validates the tools-first approach. They proved contractors will pay monthly for useful software. FTW offers similar tools at a lower price point with AI estimation and a marketplace on top.

### Porch

Porch's pivot is the most dramatic in the industry and serves as a validation case for FTW's thesis.

**Pivot timeline:**
- 2013-2018: Porch launches as a HomeAdvisor competitor, contractor marketplace and lead-gen platform.
- 2019: Begins acquiring home warranty and insurance companies (HIC, American Home Protect).
- 2020: Goes public via SPAC at $1B+ valuation. Marketplace revenue already declining.
- 2021-2022: Acquires multiple insurance businesses. Insurance revenue surpasses marketplace revenue.
- 2023-2024: Insurance becomes 67% of total revenue. Lead-gen is an afterthought.
- 2025-2026: Porch is functionally an insurance company that happens to have a legacy contractor directory. They no longer invest meaningfully in the marketplace.

**What Porch's pivot means:** The company that had the resources, the data, and the market position to compete in contractor lead-gen decided the business model was not viable long-term. They walked away. This validates FTW's core thesis — pure lead-gen is a dying model. The future is tools-first, relationships-second, with lead generation as a byproduct of platform value, not the primary revenue driver.

### New Entrants to Watch

**Handoff** — AI-driven suite for residential contractors. Claims to replace 5+ separate software subscriptions with one platform. Most direct competitor to FTW's vision of bundled tools + AI. They do not have a marketplace component, but their AI toolset is ambitious. Based in Austin. Seed-stage funding. Watch closely — if they add a marketplace, they become a serious threat.

**Bark** — UK-based, expanding into US. Similar to Thumbtack but with a different matching model (customers describe needs, Bark finds and contacts professionals). Less established in the US but growing. Generalist platform, not construction-specific. Currently stronger in UK, Australia, and Europe than in the southern US.

**AllBetter** — Positioning as a contractor-friendly alternative to Angi. Claims zero lead fees and contractor-first design. Very early stage. Their messaging mirrors FTW's value proposition closely, which validates the market opportunity but also means FTW needs to move fast on brand establishment. No AI estimation, no QuickBooks integration. If they gain traction in the South, they could compete for the same contractor base.

## Market Direction

The pure lead-gen model is dying. Angi is shrinking. Porch ran from it entirely. Houzz pivoted to SaaS. The market is moving toward tools-first, leads-second — which is exactly FTW's model. FTW is positioned on the right side of this trend.

The winners in 2027-2028 will be platforms that provide daily-use tools (estimation, project management, scheduling, invoicing) and generate leads as a natural byproduct of platform engagement. Thumbtack is the one exception that continues to grow on a lead-gen model, but even they are investing heavily in tools and AI partnerships to add value beyond lead delivery.

## Competitive Response Playbook

What FTW does if each competitor enters or intensifies in Mississippi:

**If Angi aggressively targets Mississippi:**
- Lean into the anti-lead-fee messaging. Every contractor in the state who has been burned by Angi is a potential FTW convert. Run targeted campaigns with real cost comparisons ($135/lead vs. $0/lead).
- Emphasize QuickBooks-native payments — "your money goes to YOUR QuickBooks, not their escrow."
- Angi's brand damage among contractors is our recruiting advantage. We do not need to attack them; contractors already distrust them.

**If Thumbtack intensifies Mississippi presence:**
- Compete on construction depth. Thumbtack is a generalist. FTW has ConstructionAI, construction-specific project management, and trade-specific tools they cannot match without massive investment.
- Highlight lead quality. Thumbtack leads include everyone from DIY price-shoppers to serious homeowners. FTW's matching algorithm (when built) will prioritize quality over quantity.
- Offer migration incentives — free Pro tier trial for any contractor who shows their Thumbtack spend history.

**If Houzz Pro expands aggressively:**
- Differentiate on price ($49/mo vs. $85-399/mo) and AI estimation. Houzz Pro has no AI estimation capability.
- Houzz's user base skews design-build. FTW targets a broader contractor base including trades (electrical, plumbing, concrete, framing) that Houzz does not serve well.

**If Handoff launches a marketplace:**
- Move fast on contractor acquisition in Mississippi and Texas. First-mover advantage in a local market is durable.
- Emphasize QuickBooks-native payments and the FairTrade Promise — trust signals that take time to build.
- Monitor their AI capabilities closely. If their estimation engine is comparable to ConstructionAI, differentiate on marketplace network effects and local market knowledge.

**If AllBetter targets the South:**
- Brand establishment is the defense. AllBetter's messaging is similar to FTW's, so the winner is whoever gets to contractors first with a working product.
- FTW's AI estimation is a hard technical moat. AllBetter has no AI and building one requires construction-specific training data they do not have.
- James's personal relationships with Mississippi contractors are an advantage no outside startup can replicate.

**General defensive principles:**
1. Lock in contractors with useful free tools before competitors arrive. Switching costs increase with usage.
2. QuickBooks integration is a moat — once a contractor's invoicing flows through FTW-to-QuickBooks, switching is painful.
3. ConstructionAI accuracy improves with more local data. Every estimate generated in Mississippi makes the model harder to replicate.
4. The FairTrade Promise and zero-lead-fee brand positioning should be established before competitors can copy the messaging.
