# Construction Labor Shortage

## National Numbers (2026)
- Industry needs **349,000 net new workers** in 2026
- Rising to **456,000 in 2027**
- **92% of construction firms** report difficulty finding workers
- **88%** of firms hiring craft workers have unfilled openings
- Labor costs projected to rise **6-8% annually**
- Total US construction workforce: **8.3 million** — needs to reach **8.9 million** by 2028 to meet demand

## Impact on Project Timelines and Pricing

### Timeline Expansion
- Average residential project timeline has increased **22%** since 2020 due to labor constraints
- A project that took 3 weeks in 2020 now takes nearly 4 weeks
- **Electrician and plumber wait times**: Homeowners report waiting **3-6 weeks** just to get a licensed electrician or plumber on-site in many markets
- **Cascading delays**: When one trade is late, it pushes back every subsequent trade. A 2-week plumber delay on a bathroom remodel can extend the total project by 4+ weeks.

### Pricing Pressure
- Labor costs now represent **40-50%** of total residential project cost (up from 35-40% in 2019)
- Skilled trade rates increasing **6-8% annually** — faster than material inflation
- **Bid inflation**: 70% of contractors pad bids to account for the risk of not finding subcontractors. Average bid padding: **8-12%**.
- In tight labor markets, homeowners are seeing quotes **15-25% higher** than historical norms for equivalent work
- Contractors who can accurately estimate labor costs (using tools like ConstructionAI) can bid more competitively while protecting margins

## Root Causes
- **Aging workforce + accelerated retirements** (structural, not cyclical)
  - Average age of a construction worker: **42.5 years** (2025)
  - **41%** of the current workforce will retire within the next 10 years
  - The industry loses **~500,000 workers to retirement annually** and only replaces **~350,000**
- **"Experience cliff"** — older workers departing takes institutional knowledge with them
  - A 30-year master electrician carries estimation knowledge, code familiarity, and problem-solving skills that take decades to rebuild
  - AI tools like ConstructionAI can partially capture and democratize this institutional knowledge
- **Immigration uncertainty** reducing labor supply
  - Immigrant workers represent **~25%** of the US construction workforce nationally
  - Policy changes have reduced new immigrant worker availability by an estimated **8-12%** since 2020
- **Younger workers not entering trades**
  - Only **3%** of high school students express interest in construction careers
  - "Go to college" cultural pressure has directed two generations away from trades
  - Social media is beginning to shift perception (trade content creators showing income potential)

## Trade School Enrollment Trends

### Recent Data
- Trade school enrollment increased **16%** from 2020-2025 — the first sustained growth in decades
- Electrical and HVAC programs seeing the biggest enrollment increases (**22%** and **19%** respectively)
- Plumbing program enrollment up **14%**
- General construction/carpentry programs up **11%**
- Average trade school completion time: **6-24 months** (vs. 4 years for a degree)
- Average starting salary for licensed trade graduates: **$45,000-$55,000** — competitive with many bachelor's degree holders

### But It's Not Enough
- Even with enrollment increases, trade schools are producing **~120,000 graduates/year** against a deficit of **350,000-450,000** workers
- The math doesn't close: even at current growth rates, the shortage persists through at least **2032**
- This is a structural tailwind for FTW — labor scarcity makes contractor time MORE valuable, which makes tools that save time MORE necessary

## Mississippi Specific

### Wage Gap
- Mississippi average construction worker pay: **$37,180** (near bottom nationally)
- National average: **$48,210**
- **Mississippi pays 23% below the national average** for construction labor
- Low wages make attracting workers harder in MS vs. higher-paying states
- Workers with mobility (especially younger, licensed tradespeople) leave Mississippi for higher-paying markets in Tennessee, Texas, and the Carolinas
- This creates an even tighter labor market for Mississippi specifically — fewer workers, competing with neighboring states that pay more

- Mississippi contractors who can't match Tennessee/Texas wages need other tools to compete for workers: better scheduling, less wasted time, professional systems. FTW helps here.

### AWS Data Center Impact
- AWS data center project in Madison County will absorb **4,000-6,000 construction workers**
- Project timeline: **2025-2028** (peak labor absorption in 2026-2027)
- Madison County is **~160 miles** south of Oxford — far enough that it won't directly pull Oxford workers, but the ripple effect matters
- **Ripple effects**: Contractors from Jackson, Meridian, and central Mississippi will be pulled toward the AWS project, leaving vacancies that central MS homeowners will struggle to fill. Some of those homeowners may look further (including Oxford contractors) for availability.
- Large infrastructure projects also raise wage expectations across the state — even Oxford contractors may need to raise rates to stay competitive

### Immigration Policy Effects on Mississippi
- Mississippi construction workforce is an estimated **12-15%** immigrant labor (below the 25% national average)
- Mississippi's agricultural sector competes with construction for the same immigrant labor pool
- E-Verify requirements (Mississippi was an early adopter) have reduced the undocumented labor pool more than in states without mandatory verification
- Any further immigration restrictions disproportionately impact Southern states where construction demand is growing fastest
- Mississippi's immigrant construction workers are concentrated in **drywall, roofing, concrete, and landscaping** — trades that are already short-staffed

## Are Contractors Looking for Efficiency Tools?

### The Productivity Imperative
- **93%** of contractors agree technology can significantly increase productivity
- With fewer workers available, contractors must do more with the workers they have
- A contractor who can estimate in 10 minutes (with AI) instead of 2 hours (manually) can take on **3-4 more bids per week**
- Better scheduling tools reduce downtime between jobs — even **30 minutes saved per day** equals **130 hours/year** of recovered productive time

### What Contractors Want From Tools
- **#1**: Save me time on estimation and paperwork (71%)
- **#2**: Help me find and win more jobs (64%)
- **#3**: Get me paid faster (58%)
- **#4**: Reduce scheduling conflicts (45%)
- **#5**: Make me look more professional to customers (38%)

### Adoption Barriers
- **"I don't have time to learn new software"** (52%) — biggest barrier
- **"Too expensive for what I get"** (34%)
- **"I've tried tools before and they didn't work"** (28%)
- **"My current system works fine"** (22%)
- The "no time to learn" barrier is why FTW's onboarding must be sub-15-minutes and the time-to-value must be immediate

## FTW Implications
- **Labor shortage makes efficient contractor matching MORE valuable, not less.** When there are fewer contractors, connecting the right contractor to the right job at the right time matters more.
- **Contractors who are busy need better tools** (estimation, scheduling, invoicing) to handle volume. A solo operator doing 15 jobs/month manually is at capacity. With FTW tools, that same operator could handle 18-20 jobs/month.
- **Homeowners struggling to find available contractors (25% cite this)** need a marketplace to discover who has capacity. FTW can surface "availability" as a marketplace feature — show which contractors can start soonest.
- **Licensed trades (electricians, plumbers, HVAC) should be FTW's priority for contractor onboarding.** They have the worst shortage, the highest homeowner demand, and the most to gain from efficiency tools.
- **The AWS mega-project in Madison County** could pull workers away from residential work statewide — something to monitor. If residential contractors get busier because commercial is absorbing labor, FTW's value proposition strengthens.
- **Mississippi's wage gap means FTW has a retention angle**: "Make more money per hour by reducing wasted time on estimation and admin." A contractor who saves 5 hours/week on paperwork and estimation can reinvest that time in billable work — effectively a raise without the contractor charging more.
- **Trade school enrollment is growing but not fast enough.** The shortage persists through at least 2032. FTW is building for a market where contractor time becomes increasingly scarce and valuable — this is a long-term structural advantage.
- **The "no time to learn" barrier (52%)** is the biggest threat to FTW adoption. Onboarding must be dead simple: sign up, connect QuickBooks, get your first AI estimate in under 15 minutes. Every additional step increases abandonment.
