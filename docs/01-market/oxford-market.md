# Oxford, MS Market Analysis

## Home Prices (2026)
- Average home value: **$400,674** (up 10.8% YoY)
- Median home price: **$495,000**
- Single-family homes average: **$540,000**
- Condos average: **$529,900**
- Days on market: **~23 days** (seller's market)
- Inventory: **133 homes** available (tight supply)
- Months of supply: **3.1** (anything under 4 = seller's market)

## Ole Miss University Impact

### Housing Demand Drivers
- University of Mississippi enrollment: **~22,000 students** (2025) — largest employer in Lafayette County
- Annual faculty/staff: **~3,200** — many are homeowners who invest in property upkeep
- Student housing demand drives constant rental property renovation. Landlords upgrade units between leases (May-August) to compete for tenants.
- University construction projects (dorms, athletic facilities, academic buildings) keep commercial contractors busy and raise the general skill floor in the area
- Game-day economy drives commercial renovation: restaurants, bars, and retail along the Square and University Ave cycle through remodels every 3-5 years
- **Parent-funded housing**: Ole Miss attracts out-of-state students with means. Parents buying condos/townhomes for students creates a secondary renovation market

### Population & Growth
- Oxford city population: **~28,000** (2025 estimate)
- Lafayette County population: **~57,000**
- Oxford has grown **~18%** over the past decade — one of the fastest-growing cities in Mississippi
- University presence provides economic stability that most small Mississippi cities lack
- Median household income (Lafayette County): **~$48,500** — below national median but boosted by university-connected households
- Homeownership rate: **~55%** (lower than MS average of 72% due to large student renter population)
- Among non-student households, homeownership is closer to **70%**

## Seasonal Construction Patterns in Mississippi

### Peak Season (March - June)
- Highest construction activity: mild weather, pre-hurricane season, pre-summer heat
- Contractors are most booked during this window
- Best time for FTW to demonstrate value — contractors need efficiency tools when they're busiest

### Summer Slowdown (July - August)
- Mississippi heat index regularly exceeds **110F** — outdoor work slows significantly
- Interior work (kitchens, bathrooms, painting) continues
- Rental property turnover: landlords renovating student housing between leases
- Many contractors use this time for smaller jobs, maintenance, and catch-up

### Hurricane Season (June - November)
- Mississippi is not coastal but gets significant wind and rain damage from Gulf storms
- Post-storm demand spikes create urgent need for roofing, siding, tree damage, and water remediation contractors
- Storm chasers (out-of-state contractors) flood Mississippi markets after major events — FTW's local verification becomes a trust differentiator
- Insurance claim work surges: contractors need estimation tools that produce documentation insurers accept

### Fall Recovery (September - November)
- Second peak season as temperatures cool
- Holiday-driven remodels (homeowners want kitchens/bathrooms done before Thanksgiving/Christmas)
- Ole Miss football season drives commercial renovation deadlines on the Square

### Winter (December - February)
- Slowest season but Mississippi winters are mild (average lows: 30-40F)
- Outdoor work continues in Mississippi unlike northern states
- Contractors use downtime for planning, bidding, and lining up spring projects
- Good window for FTW onboarding and training — contractors have more time to learn new tools

## Lafayette County Demographics

### Income Distribution
- Households earning $75K+: **~28%** (primary renovation spending bracket)
- Households earning $50-75K: **~18%**
- Households earning under $50K: **~54%** (includes student households)
- When filtering out student-only households, the income profile shifts significantly upward
- University-connected households (faculty, administrators, medical center staff) have stable incomes and are more likely to invest in home improvement

### Age Distribution (Non-Student)
- 25-44: **~30%** — first-time homeowners, starting renovations
- 45-64: **~28%** — peak renovation spending years, established in homes
- 65+: **~15%** — aging-in-place modifications, accessibility upgrades
- The 25-44 and 45-64 brackets represent **~58%** of non-student adults and are FTW's primary homeowner demographic

### Housing Stock
- Median year of construction for Oxford homes: **~1990**
- Homes built before 1980: **~25%** — likely need major systems updates (electrical, plumbing, HVAC)
- Homes built 1980-2005: **~40%** — entering the "big renovation" cycle (20-40 years old)
- New construction (2005-present): **~35%** — mostly maintenance and cosmetic updates
- **65% of Oxford's housing stock** is at an age where significant renovation work is likely within the next 5 years

## Neighborhoods with Highest Renovation Activity

### The Square / Downtown
- Historic commercial district — restaurants, retail, and office spaces cycling through remodels
- Mixed-use properties with residential above commercial
- Historic preservation requirements add complexity (and cost) to renovation projects
- FTW estimation tools that account for historic preservation surcharges would be valuable here

### Eagle Pointe / Country Club Area
- Higher-end residential ($500K-$1M+)
- Kitchen and bathroom remodels, outdoor living spaces, pools
- Homeowners in this bracket hire professionals — not DIY territory

### College Hill / North Lamar
- Established neighborhoods with 1970s-1990s housing stock
- Full gut renovations, additions, and major system replacements
- Mix of long-term residents and university-connected buyers

### Highway 6 Corridor (East/West)
- Rapid new development — apartments, townhomes, retail
- Contractor demand for new construction AND renovation of adjacent older neighborhoods
- Commercial build-outs for new businesses moving into the corridor

### Student Housing Areas (University Ave, Jackson Ave)
- High turnover, frequent cosmetic renovations
- Landlords are repeat customers — once onboarded, they bring consistent volume
- Smaller project sizes ($2,000-$8,000) but high frequency

## Local Competitor Landscape

### What Oxford Contractors Currently Use
- **QuickBooks**: Most common accounting tool. Many contractors use QuickBooks Online for invoicing but not estimation.
- **Paper/Excel estimates**: The majority of small Oxford contractors still estimate on paper, in Excel, or using basic Word templates. Manual estimation is the norm.
- **Facebook Marketplace / Groups**: Oxford has active "Oxford MS Contractors" and "Lafayette County Home Services" Facebook groups where contractors get leads. Free but unstructured.
- **Word of mouth**: Dominant lead source. Oxford is small enough that reputation travels fast.
- **Angi / HomeAdvisor**: Some contractors use these but complain about lead quality and cost. Several Oxford contractors have publicly left these platforms.
- **Google Business Profile**: Contractors with GBP listings get organic leads, but most Oxford contractors have incomplete or unoptimized profiles.
- **Buildertrend / CoConstruct**: A handful of larger Oxford builders use these, but the $99-$399/mo price point is too high for most solo operators.
- **Thumbtack**: Limited penetration in Oxford. More common in larger metros.

### Competitive Gaps in Oxford
- No local construction marketplace exists for Oxford specifically
- No tool combines estimation + lead generation + invoicing at a price point accessible to solo operators
- The Facebook groups are free but have no verification, no estimation, no payment processing
- Contractors who want professional tools are forced to cobble together 3-5 separate subscriptions

## Building Permits (Lafayette County)
- Residential building permits: base fee **$150 + $0.30/sqft** living area
- Lafayette County requires permits for ALL residential construction work (since 2022)
- Building code: **2012 International Building Code**
- Enforced by Lafayette County Building & Planning Department
- Permit data is a potential FTW feature: help contractors pull and track permits through the platform

## FTW Implications
- **Seller's market** = homeowners investing in existing homes (remodeling over buying new). This is FTW's core market.
- **High home values ($400K+)** justify professional contractor work, not DIY. The average Oxford homeowner can afford FTW's marketplace fees.
- **Active permit activity** = contractors are busy and need efficiency tools.
- **Tight inventory** means more renovation work as people improve rather than move.
- **Ole Miss creates a stable, recurring demand floor** — student housing turnover, university projects, and faculty homeownership all contribute. FTW should target landlords as early adopters (repeat customers with consistent volume).
- **Seasonal patterns matter for launch timing**: Launch in February-March to catch contractors heading into peak season. They're planning work, need tools, and have a few weeks to learn the platform before the rush hits.
- **Storm season is a marketing moment**: After any significant weather event, FTW can position as "find verified local contractors, not storm chasers." This messaging resonates deeply in Mississippi.
- **Oxford's size is an advantage for validation**: With ~150 active residential contractors, FTW can realistically talk to or demo for a significant percentage of the market. Personal outreach works here — it won't scale in Nashville, but it will in Oxford.
- **Facebook groups are the current "marketplace"**: FTW's initial marketing should target these groups. Many Oxford contractors are already looking for leads online — they just don't have a structured platform.
- **The 65% of housing stock entering renovation age** means demand will increase over the next 5 years regardless of economic conditions. FTW is launching into a structural tailwind in Oxford.
