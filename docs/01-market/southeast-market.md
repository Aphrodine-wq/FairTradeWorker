# Southeast US Construction Market

## Regional Dominance
- Southeast held **41.12%** of US residential construction market share in 2025 — the dominant region
- Characterized by robust civil infrastructure, healthcare expansion, military projects
- Remodeling Market Index (RMI) above break-even (50) for **24 consecutive quarters**
- Southeast residential construction spending: **~$369 billion** (41% of $900B national)
- The Southeast is the only US region where residential construction market share has GROWN every year since 2018

## Market Drivers
- **Aging housing stock** — older homes need renovation (structural tailwind)
- **Lock-in effect** — homeowners with low mortgage rates renovate instead of moving
- **Aging-in-place** — older homeowners modifying homes rather than relocating
- **Population growth** — Southeast continues to attract domestic migration
- **Lower cost of living** — attracting remote workers and retirees from higher-cost regions
- **Business-friendly tax environments** — driving commercial construction (offices, warehouses, data centers)
- **Climate resilience investment** — hurricane hardening, flood mitigation, and energy efficiency upgrades

## State-by-State Comparison

### Licensing Requirements
| Factor | Mississippi | Tennessee | Alabama |
|--------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| General contractor license | State-level, required for projects >$50K | State-level, required for all projects >$25K | State + municipal, required for projects >$50K |
| Specialty trade licenses | Required for electrical, plumbing, HVAC | Required for electrical, plumbing, HVAC, gas | Required for electrical, plumbing, HVAC |
| License reciprocity | Limited — no automatic reciprocity with neighbors | Limited — some reciprocity with border states | Limited reciprocity |
| Insurance requirements | GL + WC required (WC for 5+ employees) | GL required, WC for 5+ employees | GL required, WC for 5+ employees |
| Building code | 2012 IBC (statewide, but local enforcement varies) | 2018 IBC (statewide adoption) | No statewide code — county-by-county |
| License exam difficulty | Moderate (PSI-administered) | Moderate (PSI-administered) | Moderate-Hard (varies by county) |
| Continuing education | Not required for GC license renewal | 4 hours/year required | Varies by municipality |

### FTW Licensing Implications
- **Mississippi first**: Licensing requirements are straightforward and well-documented. FTW can verify contractor licenses against state records.
- **Tennessee second**: Similar structure to MS, slightly more complex. Memphis expansion is feasible with minor adjustments to the verification system.
- **Alabama is the wildcard**: No statewide building code means county-by-county rules. FTW expansion into Alabama requires per-county configuration. Birmingham and Huntsville have their own frameworks.
- FTW's verification system should be designed to handle state-level AND municipal-level licensing from Day 1 to avoid rework during expansion.

## Climate and Weather Impact on Construction

### Hurricane Season (June - November)
- Mississippi has 70 miles of Gulf coastline but inland areas (including Oxford) still experience storm impacts: wind damage, flooding, fallen trees
- **Post-hurricane demand surge**: After a major storm, roofing, siding, and water remediation demand can spike **300-500%** in affected areas
- Insurance claim work requires detailed documentation — FTW's estimation tools that produce professional, itemized breakdowns are valuable for insurance submissions
- **Storm chasers** (out-of-state contractors who follow hurricanes) create trust problems. FTW's local verification is a direct counter to this — "hire a verified local contractor, not a storm chaser"

### Humidity and Moisture
- Southeast humidity creates unique construction challenges: mold prevention, moisture barriers, ventilation requirements
- HVAC systems work harder in the Southeast — shorter replacement cycles (12-15 years vs. 18-20 in northern states)
- **Exterior paint** has shorter lifespans in humid climates (5-7 years vs. 8-10 in dry climates) — more frequent repainting jobs
- ConstructionAI's estimation models should account for Southeast-specific material choices (moisture-resistant drywall, treated lumber, vapor barriers)

### Termites
- Mississippi is in **Termite Infestation Probability Zone 1** (very heavy) — the highest risk zone in the US
- Annual termite damage costs Mississippi homeowners an estimated **$500M+**
- Pest control and termite remediation is a recurring construction-adjacent service
- Termite damage repair (structural wood replacement, foundation repair) is a consistent revenue stream for contractors
- FTW could partner with pest control companies for referral revenue or include termite-related trades in the marketplace

### Heat and Worker Safety
- Mississippi averages **85-100F** from June through September with heat index regularly exceeding **110F**
- OSHA heat illness prevention standards affect outdoor construction scheduling
- Crews typically start at **6:00-6:30 AM** and stop outdoor work by **1:00-2:00 PM** during peak summer
- This compressed workday reduces productivity by **20-30%** during summer months
- FTW's scheduling tools should account for seasonal workday length differences

## Infrastructure Spending Driving Commercial Construction

### Federal and State Projects (2025-2030)
- **Infrastructure Investment and Jobs Act (IIJA)**: Mississippi allocated **$5.6 billion** for highways, bridges, water systems, and broadband
- **Mississippi highway spending**: $1.2B annually for maintenance and new construction
- **Bridge repairs**: 1,386 Mississippi bridges rated "structurally deficient" — federal funding is repairing these through 2030
- **Broadband expansion**: $1.2B for rural broadband infrastructure — requires construction crews for installation

## Expansion Markets (After Oxford)

### Tier 1: Adjacent Markets (Year 1-2)
- **Tupelo, MS** — 80 miles east. Mid-size market, growing. Similar contractor dynamics to Oxford. Low competition. Can share marketing materials and processes from Oxford launch.
- **Memphis, TN** — 90 miles north of Oxford. Large market, established contractor base. FTW can start with the MS side of the metro (DeSoto County, Southaven, Olive Branch) before crossing into TN.

### Tier 2: Growth Markets (Year 2-3)
- **Nashville, TN** — Top-5 real estate market. 1.5-2% annual population growth. Oracle campus bringing 8,500 jobs. Hot market but higher competition and higher CAC. Requires paid acquisition — different model than Oxford's grassroots approach.
- **Huntsville, AL** — Fastest-growing metro in the Southeast. Defense/aerospace workforce is tech-savvy and expects digital tools. Underserved by construction marketplaces.
- **Jackson, MS** — State capital. Larger contractor base, more competition, but declining population makes this a lower priority.

### Tier 3: Scale Markets (Year 3+)
- **Birmingham, AL** — Growing Southeast hub. Medical sector driving commercial and residential construction.
- **Chattanooga, TN** — Tech corridor, growing. Smaller market but high average income.
- **Gulf Coast MS** (Biloxi/Gulfport) — Hurricane-driven demand, insurance renovation work.

- **Climate creates unique marketplace opportunities**: hurricane response contractor matching, heat-season interior project focus, and termite damage repair are all Southeast-specific use cases that national platforms don't address well. FTW can build regional features that national competitors won't.
- **Facebook Groups are the primary competition in Mississippi** — not Angi, not Buildertrend. FTW's go-to-market in Oxford should directly target the Facebook group audience with a "same community, better tools" message.
