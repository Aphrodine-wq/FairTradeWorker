# Contractor Demographics & Persona

## Who Is the FTW Contractor?
- **Total US general contractors:** 35,625+
- **Total US specialty trade contractors:** ~340,000 firms
- **Construction workforce:** 8.3 million (2025)
- **Age:** Skews older. Industry faces an "experience cliff" as senior workers retire, taking institutional knowledge with them.
  - Average age: **42.5 years**
  - Under 25: **9%** of workforce
  - 25-34: **21%**
  - 35-44: **24%**
  - 45-54: **23%**
  - 55+: **23%** (largest single cohort for business owners)
- **Mindset:** 42% describe their business as a "calling or life's passion." 61% are first-generation entrepreneurs. These are people who care deeply about their work.
- **Gender:** 89% male, 11% female (female-owned construction firms growing at 2x the rate of male-owned)
- **Race/ethnicity:** 63% white, 30% Hispanic/Latino, 6% Black, 1% other — Mississippi has a higher percentage of Black-owned construction businesses (~14%) than the national average
- **Income:** Mississippi average construction pay: $37,180 (bottom nationally). Small residential contractors in Oxford likely $50-80K.
- **QuickBooks adoption:** Construction is the #1 industry for QuickBooks at 17.22% of all QB users. 62% market share overall. FTW's QuickBooks-native strategy hits the exact tool most contractors already use.

## Breakdown by Company Size

### Solo Operators (1 person) — 65% of residential contractors
- Revenue: **$75,000-$180,000/year** gross (varies widely by trade and market)
- Net income after expenses: **$45,000-$90,000**
- No employees, may use 1-2 subcontractors on larger jobs
- Wears every hat: estimator, salesperson, project manager, laborer, bookkeeper
- **Most time-constrained** — estimation and admin eat into billable hours
- **Least likely to use sophisticated software** — often paper/Excel/phone notes
- **Most likely to benefit from FTW** — all-in-one tool that replaces 3-5 separate apps
- Typical monthly software spend: **$50-$150** (QuickBooks + maybe one other tool)

### Small Crews (2-5 employees) — 25% of residential contractors
- Revenue: **$250,000-$750,000/year** gross
- Net income (owner): **$75,000-$150,000**
- Owner still does hands-on work but starting to delegate
- Usually has a "right hand" — a lead carpenter/foreman who runs the crew
- **Beginning to feel the pain of disorganized systems** — miscommunication with crew, estimates that don't match actuals, scheduling conflicts
- More likely to use project management tools: **38%** use some form of digital scheduling
- Typical monthly software spend: **$150-$400**

### Mid-Size Firms (6-20 employees) — 8% of residential contractors
- Revenue: **$1M-$5M/year** gross
- Owner is transitioning from "doing the work" to "running the business"
- Has dedicated roles: office manager, estimator, project managers, crew leads
- **Already using industry software** — Buildertrend, CoConstruct, or Procore
- Less likely to switch platforms unless FTW offers significant advantages
- FTW's Pro/Enterprise tier with team features would target this segment
- Typical monthly software spend: **$400-$1,500**

### Large Firms (20+ employees) — 2% of residential contractors
- Revenue: **$5M+/year** gross
- Enterprise operations with established software stacks
- **Not FTW's target market** — they use Procore, Oracle Primavera, or custom solutions
- FTW may eventually serve these firms as a marketplace partner (to source subcontractors), not as a SaaS customer

## Technology Comfort by Age Bracket

### Under 35 (Digital Natives)
- **Smartphone-first**: 98% use smartphones for work
- **App-comfortable**: average of 6-8 work apps, willing to try new ones
- **Social media active**: use Instagram/TikTok to showcase work and find leads
- **Expect modern UX**: if FTW feels like software from 2010, they'll bounce
- **Fastest adopters** but also the **quickest to churn** if the tool doesn't deliver value immediately
- **22%** of this group — small but growing and influential

### 35-50 (Pragmatic Adopters)
- **Smartphone + tablet**: 95% smartphone, 70% tablet on-site
- **Selective about tools**: will try new software but only if the value proposition is clear and specific
- Use QuickBooks, email, and texting as primary digital tools
- Comfortable with apps but not excited by them — "show me the ROI, not the features"
- **47%** of this group — FTW's core adoption demographic
- **Key conversion trigger**: "this will save you X hours per week on estimation"

### Over 50 (Reluctant Adopters)
- **Smartphone**: 85% have one, but **many only use it for calls, texts, and photos**
- **31%** still use paper for estimation and recordkeeping
- **QuickBooks Desktop** (not Online) is common in this bracket — transitioning to cloud is a barrier
- **Referral-dependent**: most of their leads come from 20+ years of reputation. They don't feel the need for a marketplace.
- Will adopt if: (a) their kids/office manager sets it up, (b) they see a peer using it successfully, or (c) a homeowner requests FTW specifically
- **31%** of this group — not early adopters, but important for marketplace completeness

### Key Insight
- **62% already on QuickBooks** validates FTW's QuickBooks-native strategy. FTW doesn't need to convince contractors to use new accounting software — it integrates with what they already have.
- **48% using Excel for estimation** is FTW's direct replacement target. ConstructionAI replaces the Excel spreadsheet with AI-powered estimation that flows into QuickBooks invoicing.
- **22% still on paper** represents a digital transformation opportunity but also the hardest segment to onboard. Start with the Excel/QB users, let paper users follow.

## Mobile/Tech Adoption
- **93% of contractors use smartphones** on the job
- **65% use tablets** on-site
- **Frontline workers use 5+ work apps** on average (80% increase in last 2 years)
- **BYOD is standard** — contractors use their personal phones for work
- Construction is now **mobile-first**, not desktop-first. FTW must be responsive and fast on phones.
- Most-used apps on the jobsite: (1) Camera, (2) Messaging/text, (3) Calculator, (4) QuickBooks, (5) Weather
- **WiFi/connectivity on job sites**: 42% of job sites have no WiFi — FTW needs offline functionality or graceful degradation for mobile use on-site

## Truck vs. Office: Where Contractors Work

### The "Rolling Office"
- **78%** of solo and small-crew contractors do not have a dedicated office space
- Their truck IS their office: estimates written in the cab, phone calls on the road, invoices sent from the parking lot
- **Average daily drive time**: 45-90 minutes between job sites
- Contractors check email and apps during **lunch breaks (12:00-1:00 PM)** and **after hours (6:00-8:00 PM)**
- This means FTW's notification system needs to respect work hours — push notifications during active work hours are less likely to be acted on than during lunch or evening

### The Home Office
- **15%** of small contractors have a home office where they do admin work (estimation, invoicing, bookkeeping)
- Admin work typically happens **between 6:00-8:00 PM** on weekday evenings or **Sunday afternoons**
- This is when FTW usage will peak for admin tasks — the platform should perform well during these off-hours

### Dedicated Office
- **7%** of residential contractors (mostly mid-size firms with 10+ employees) have a commercial office
- These firms have administrative staff who handle estimation and bookkeeping during business hours
- FTW's team features and admin roles serve this segment

## Typical Workday Schedule

### Residential Contractor Daily Pattern
| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 5:30-6:00 AM | Wake up, check messages/email, review day's schedule |
| 6:00-6:30 AM | Load truck, drive to first job site |
| 6:30-7:00 AM | Arrive on-site, prep work area, brief crew |
| 7:00 AM-12:00 PM | Active construction work |
| 12:00-1:00 PM | Lunch break — **primary phone/app usage window** |
| 1:00-4:00 PM | Continue construction work (may leave early in summer heat) |
| 4:00-5:00 PM | Clean up, drive home or to next day's site |
| 5:00-6:00 PM | Return calls, respond to messages |
| 6:00-8:00 PM | Admin work: estimation, invoicing, scheduling, bookkeeping |
| 8:00 PM+ | Personal time (but many contractors check messages until 9-10 PM) |

### Weekly Pattern
- **Monday-Friday**: active construction work
- **Saturday**: 60% of contractors work at least a half day (especially during peak season)
- **Sunday**: mostly off, but 35% do admin/planning on Sunday evenings
- **Estimation and quoting** overwhelmingly happens in the **evening and Sunday** — this is FTW's peak engagement window

## FTW Implications
- **FTW's target user is a 30-55 year old residential contractor** in Oxford who:
  - Already uses QuickBooks
  - Has a smartphone and uses 5+ apps for work
  - Spends $58K/year on tools but wastes $11K of it
  - Is frustrated by fragmented systems and payment delays
  - Considers their work a calling, not just a job
  - Will adopt new tools IF the value is obvious and onboarding is fast
- **Solo operators (65% of the market)** are FTW's primary target. They have the most pain (wearing every hat), the lowest current software spend (easiest to justify $49/mo), and the most to gain from all-in-one tools.
- **Evening and Sunday are peak estimation hours.** FTW's AI estimation must be fast and available 24/7 — contractors are doing their "office work" when the rest of the world is off the clock.
- **The truck is the office.** FTW's mobile experience isn't a nice-to-have — it's the primary interface for 78% of target users. Every feature must work on a phone screen in a truck cab.
- **QuickBooks integration is not a feature — it's a requirement.** 62% of contractors already use it. FTW's QuickBooks-native approach means zero friction on accounting. Estimates flow to invoices flow to payments, all in the tool they already know.
- **Age-based adoption strategy**: Lead with 30-45 year olds (pragmatic adopters), let them evangelize to older contractors through peer proof. Don't spend marketing dollars trying to convert paper-only contractors over 55 — they'll come if their peers are on FTW.
- **Offline capability matters.** 42% of job sites have no WiFi. If a contractor can't pull up a saved estimate on-site without connectivity, FTW fails the basic usability test.
