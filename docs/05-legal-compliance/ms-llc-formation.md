# Mississippi LLC Formation

If FTW doesn't have a legal entity yet, it needs one immediately for: SBIR application, Innovate MS engagement, contractor agreements, platform insurance, and QuickBooks marketplace listing.

---

## Formation Details

- **Filing:** Certificate of Formation with MS Secretary of State
- **Cost:** $50 filing fee ($53.14 with processing)
- **Timeline:** Online filing only. Often approved same day or within 24 hours. Max 3-5 business days.
- **Name requirement:** Must include "LLC" or "Limited Liability Company." Must be distinguishable from existing businesses on file.
- **Annual report:** Free, due April 15th each year
- **Total first-year cost:** $75-$374 depending on registered agent choice
- **File at:** [Mississippi Secretary of State Business Portal](https://www.sos.ms.gov/business-services-regulation)

## Registered Agent Requirements

Every Mississippi LLC must designate a registered agent — a person or entity authorized to receive legal documents (lawsuits, government notices) on behalf of the LLC.

**Options:**

| Option | Cost | Pros | Cons |
|--------|------|------|------|
| James as registered agent | Free | No cost, simple | James's personal address becomes public record. Must be available at that address during business hours to receive service of process. |
| Commercial registered agent | $50-$300/year | Professional address, privacy, forwards documents promptly | Annual cost |
| Lawyer as registered agent | $100-$500/year | Legal expertise, professional handling | Higher cost |

**Recommended:** Use a commercial registered agent ($50-100/year) to keep James's home address off public records. Services like Northwest Registered Agent, Incfile, or LegalZoom offer this.

**Requirements for a registered agent:**
- Must have a physical address in Mississippi (no P.O. boxes)
- Must be available during normal business hours to accept service of process
- If using yourself, you must list a physical address — this goes on the Secretary of State's public database

## EIN Application Process

An Employer Identification Number (EIN) is required for: opening a business bank account, filing taxes, hiring employees or contractors, and registering with QuickBooks.

**How to apply:**
1. Go to IRS.gov --> "Apply for an Employer Identification Number (EIN)"
2. Select: LLC, single-member (or multi-member if applicable)
3. Responsible party: James Walton (SSN required)
4. Select reason: "Started a new business"
5. Complete the online form — takes 5-10 minutes
6. EIN is issued immediately upon completion
7. Download and save the confirmation letter (CP 575) — you'll need this for banking

**Cost:** Free. Do not pay a third party to file for an EIN — the IRS provides this service at no charge.

**Timing:** Apply for the EIN immediately after the LLC is approved by the Secretary of State. You need the LLC's legal name and formation date.

## Mississippi State Tax Obligations

### Franchise Tax

- **What:** Mississippi imposes a franchise tax on all LLCs doing business in the state
- **Rate:** $2.50 per $1,000 of capital employed in Mississippi (minimum $25)
- **Due date:** March 15 each year (for calendar-year filers)
- **Filing:** Mississippi Department of Revenue Form 84-110
- **First year:** Pro-rated based on formation date
- **Note:** "Capital employed" includes total assets minus outside liabilities. For a startup with minimal assets, the minimum $25 applies.

### Income Tax

- **What:** Mississippi taxes LLC income. Single-member LLCs are disregarded entities — income passes through to James's personal return.
- **State income tax rates (2026):**
  - First $10,000: 0% (Mississippi eliminated the lowest bracket)
  - $10,001 - $15,000: 4%
  - Over $15,000: 5%
- **Filing:** Mississippi Individual Income Tax Return (Form 80-105) for single-member LLC pass-through
- **Estimated taxes:** If FTW expects to owe $200+ in state taxes, quarterly estimated payments are required (April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15)
- **Federal:** Single-member LLC income is reported on Schedule C of James's personal 1040

### Sales Tax

- **What:** Mississippi has a 7% sales tax, but SaaS/digital services are a gray area
- **SaaS taxability in Mississippi:** As of 2026, Mississippi does tax SaaS as a taxable service. FTW's subscription revenue is likely subject to Mississippi sales tax.
- **Action needed:** Register for a Mississippi sales tax permit with the Department of Revenue. Collect and remit 7% on subscription revenue from Mississippi customers.
- **Out-of-state customers:** Only collect if FTW has nexus in that state (physical presence or economic nexus threshold, usually $100K-$250K in sales)

## Business Bank Account Setup

Open a dedicated business bank account immediately after receiving the EIN. Never commingle personal and business funds — it can pierce the LLC's liability protection.

**What you need to open an account:**
- Certificate of Formation (from Secretary of State)
- EIN confirmation letter (CP 575 from IRS)
- Operating Agreement (even a simple one — some banks require it)
- Government-issued photo ID (James's driver's license)
- Initial deposit (varies by bank — often $25-$100)

**Bank options in Oxford, MS:**
- **BancorpSouth / Cadence Bank** — Large regional bank with good business banking, local branches in Oxford
- **Renasant Bank** — Regional bank, business-friendly, Oxford branch
- **Mercury (online)** — Popular with startups. No monthly fees, no minimum balance. Good for a tech company.
- **Relay (online)** — Free business banking designed for small businesses. No fees, multiple accounts for budgeting.

**Recommended:** Open a local bank account (Cadence or Renasant) for in-person banking needs, plus a Mercury or Relay account for online operations and payment processing.

## Operating Agreement Basics

Mississippi does not legally require an operating agreement for an LLC, but you should have one anyway. Banks may require it, and it protects James's personal liability shield.

**For a single-member LLC, the operating agreement should cover:**

1. **Member information:** James Walton, 100% ownership
2. **Management structure:** Member-managed (James makes all decisions)
3. **Capital contributions:** Initial contribution amount, future contribution rules
4. **Profit and loss allocation:** 100% to the sole member
5. **Distributions:** When and how profits are distributed to James
6. **Banking authority:** Who is authorized to sign checks and make transactions
7. **Dissolution:** What happens if James wants to close the LLC
8. **Liability protection statement:** Affirms the LLC is a separate legal entity from James personally

**If FTW later takes on partners, investors, or co-founders,** the operating agreement must be updated to address: ownership percentages, voting rights, capital call provisions, transfer restrictions, buyout provisions, and dispute resolution. Get a lawyer for a multi-member operating agreement.

## Entity Type: LLC vs S-Corp vs C-Corp

### For FTW Right Now: LLC (Single-Member)

**Why LLC is the right choice at launch:**
- Simplest to form and maintain
- Pass-through taxation — no double taxation
- Full liability protection
- Can elect S-Corp tax treatment later without changing the entity
- Minimal compliance burden (no board of directors, no shareholder meetings, no minutes)
- Mississippi filing is $50 and takes a day

### When to Consider S-Corp Election

**Trigger:** When FTW's net profit exceeds ~$40,000-$50,000/year

**Why:** S-Corp election allows James to pay himself a "reasonable salary" and take remaining profits as distributions, which are not subject to self-employment tax (15.3%). This can save $5,000-$15,000+/year in taxes.

**Example:**
- FTW nets $100K profit
- As LLC: James pays ~$15,300 in self-employment tax on the full amount
- As S-Corp: James pays himself $60K salary (SE tax on $60K = ~$9,180), takes $40K as distribution (no SE tax)
- **Savings: ~$6,120/year**

**How to elect:** File IRS Form 2553 by March 15 of the tax year you want it to take effect. The LLC stays an LLC legally — it's just taxed differently.

### When to Consider C-Corp

**Trigger:** When FTW is ready to take outside investment (VC, angel investors)

**Why:** Investors strongly prefer C-Corps because:
- Standard investment instruments (preferred stock, convertible notes) are designed for C-Corps
- No pass-through tax complications for investors
- Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) exclusion — up to $10M in capital gains can be excluded from federal tax if the stock is held 5+ years (this is huge)
- SBIR grants can be received by LLCs, but some programs and investors prefer C-Corp structure

**How to convert:** An LLC can convert to a C-Corp, but it's a taxable event and requires legal work. If FTW is heading toward serious fundraising, convert before the company has significant value (to minimize tax impact of the conversion).

### For SBIR Specifically

- SBIRs can be awarded to LLCs — there is no requirement to be a C-Corp
- However, some SBIR programs may prefer or require that the entity be a "small business concern" with specific ownership structures
- The LLC structure is fine for initial SBIR applications
- If FTW receives SBIR funding and plans to raise VC on top of it, consider converting to C-Corp at that stage
