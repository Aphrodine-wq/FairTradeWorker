# Two-Sided Marketplace Lessons

## What Kills Construction Marketplaces

1. **Chicken-and-egg.** Both sides are interdependent. Can't attract homeowners without contractors, can't attract contractors without homeowners.
2. **Disintermediation.** Users go around the platform to avoid fees (mitigated by FTW's subscription model — no per-transaction fees to dodge).
3. **Failing vendors.** Marketplaces that only optimize for buyers (homeowners) at the expense of sellers (contractors) enter a death spiral. Contractors leave, supply drops, homeowner experience degrades, homeowners leave.
4. **Over-building before validation.** 90% of two-sided marketplace apps fail within their first year, not from bad tech, but from building too much before finding product-market fit.
5. **Construction-specific complexity.** Some argue construction needs a THREE-sided marketplace (homeowner, contractor, materials/suppliers). FTW's materials partnership revenue stream may evolve into this.

## Detailed Case Studies of Failed Construction Marketplaces

### Homejoy (2012-2015) — Raised $40M, Shut Down

**What they did:** Home cleaning marketplace that expanded into home repair and handyman services. VC-backed, launched in 30+ cities.

**What went wrong:**
- Massive disintermediation — customers would use Homejoy once to find a cleaner, then hire them directly and cut out the platform
- They charged per-transaction fees (15-20%), giving both sides a financial incentive to leave
- Expanded too fast geographically before achieving density in any single market
- Worker classification lawsuits (1099 vs. W2) drained resources
- Quality control was impossible at scale — they couldn't vet service providers fast enough

**FTW lesson:** Subscription-based pricing eliminates the disintermediation incentive. Contractors pay a flat monthly fee regardless of how many jobs they win. Homeowners have no reason to leave the platform because there's no per-job fee for them either.

### Amazon Home Services (2015-2022) — Quietly Wound Down

**What they did:** Amazon tried to become the marketplace for home services, leveraging their massive consumer base.

**What went wrong:**
- Construction and home services are fundamentally local and relationship-driven — Amazon's scale advantage didn't translate
- Contractors hated Amazon's commission structure (15-20% of job value)
- Quality was inconsistent because Amazon prioritized availability over vetting
- Homeowners didn't trust Amazon for high-stakes work (a $30K kitchen remodel isn't the same as buying a $30 book)
- Amazon couldn't control the service experience the way they control product delivery

**FTW lesson:** A national brand doesn't automatically win local trust. FTW's city-by-city launch with personal contractor relationships creates trust that a faceless platform cannot. Also, the commission model is what drove contractors away — FTW's subscription model avoids this.

### Porch (2013-Present) — Pivoted Multiple Times

**What they did:** Started as a contractor-homeowner marketplace. Raised $130M+ in funding.

**What went wrong:**
- Original model was lead-gen with per-lead fees, which contractors resented (paying $50-100 for leads that often didn't convert)
- Pivoted to selling data to insurance companies (became more of a data play than a marketplace)
- Now primarily a B2B2C insurance/home services company — the original marketplace vision is dead
- Contractors were a means to an end, not the customer. When contractors felt exploited, the marketplace degraded.

**FTW lesson:** The marketplace has to serve both sides genuinely. FTW's "FairTrade" premise means contractors are the priority, not a data source. Revenue comes from voluntary subscriptions and value-added tools, not from extracting lead fees.

### HomeAdvisor/Angi (2012-Present) — Profitable But Universally Hated

**What they do:** Dominant home services lead-gen platform. $1.5B+ annual revenue (combined with Angi).

**What goes wrong:**
- Contractors pay $15-$80 per lead, and many leads are low-quality or duplicate
- Contractors report 60-80% of leads are "garbage" — tire kickers, wrong area, duplicate leads sent to 4-5 competitors simultaneously
- Homeowners often receive 5+ calls within minutes of submitting a request, creating a terrible experience
- Reviews are manipulated — Angi has been accused of suppressing negative reviews for paying contractors
- High churn: contractors sign up, get burned by lead quality, cancel within 3-6 months

**FTW lesson:** This is exactly what FTW is built to oppose. No per-lead fees. Homeowners choose who to contact, not the other way around. Transparent reviews. Contractors pay a flat subscription for tools and access, not for individual leads. The entire "FairTrade Promise" is a direct counter-positioning to the Angi model.

### Handy (2012-Present) — Survived But Struggled

**What they did:** On-demand home cleaning and handyman services marketplace. Raised $110M+.

**What went wrong:**
- Worker classification issues — treated workers as 1099 but controlled their schedules and pricing
- Race to the bottom on pricing — undercut market rates to attract homeowners, squeezing service providers
- Couldn't expand beyond cleaning/handyman into higher-value construction work
- Acquisition by Angi in 2018 effectively admitted the standalone model wasn't sustainable

**FTW lesson:** FTW does not set pricing. Contractors set their own rates, submit their own bids, and control their own business. FTW is a marketplace, not an employer. This avoids the worker classification trap and the race-to-the-bottom dynamic.

## The Three-Sided Marketplace Argument

Traditional view: construction marketplaces have two sides — homeowners and contractors.

**The reality is three sides:**

| Side | Role | What They Need |
|------|------|---------------|
| Homeowners | Demand side | Trustworthy contractors, fair pricing, quality assurance |
| Contractors | Supply side | Consistent leads, fair payment, no exploitation, tools to run their business |
| Suppliers/Materials | Input side | Volume buyers, predictable demand, logistics efficiency |

**Why the third side matters:**
- Materials are 40-60% of project cost. A marketplace that can negotiate material pricing has enormous leverage.
- Contractors waste significant time sourcing materials, comparing prices, and coordinating deliveries.
- FTW's materials partnership revenue stream positions it to eventually become this third side.

**FTW's phased approach:**
- Phase 1 (Launch): Two-sided marketplace — homeowners + contractors. Materials are the contractor's problem.
- Phase 2 (Post-PMF): Materials referral partnerships — FTW recommends suppliers and earns referral fees.
- Phase 3 (Scale): Materials integration — FTW negotiates bulk pricing, contractors order through the platform, FTW takes a margin. This is where the three-sided marketplace truly forms.

## Marketplace Health Metrics

### Liquidity Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target | What It Tells You |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| Fill rate | % of homeowner job posts that receive at least 1 contractor bid | > 80% | Whether supply is sufficient for demand |
| Time to first bid | Hours from job post to first contractor bid | < 24 hours | How responsive the supply side is |
| Match rate | % of job posts where the homeowner selects a contractor | > 40% | Whether the matches are relevant and competitive |
| Bid density | Average number of bids per job post | 3-5 | Whether there's enough competition (too few = bad match quality, too many = wasted contractor effort) |

### Engagement Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target | What It Tells You |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| Contractor WAU | Weekly active contractors (logged in + took an action) | > 60% of registered | Whether contractors find ongoing value |
| Homeowner return rate | % of homeowners who post a second job within 12 months | > 25% | Whether the experience was good enough to repeat |
| Estimation tool usage | Estimates generated per active contractor per week | > 2 | Whether the tools are sticky independent of the marketplace |
| Profile completion rate | % of contractors with fully completed profiles (photos, license, insurance) | > 80% | Whether contractors are invested in the platform |

### Economic Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target | What It Tells You |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| Free-to-Pro conversion | % of free contractors who upgrade to Pro | > 10% within 90 days | Whether Pro tier provides perceived value |
| Churn rate | % of Pro subscribers who cancel per month | < 5% monthly | Whether the value is sustained |
| Revenue per contractor | Average monthly revenue per contractor on the platform | > $30 (across free + paid) | Overall monetization health |
| CAC (contractor) | Cost to acquire a new contractor | < $50 | Whether growth is sustainable |
| CAC (homeowner) | Cost to acquire a new homeowner | < $30 | Whether demand-side growth is sustainable |

### Trust Metrics

| Metric | Definition | Target | What It Tells You |
|--------|-----------|--------|-------------------|
| NPS (contractor) | Net Promoter Score among contractors | > 40 | Whether contractors would recommend FTW |
| NPS (homeowner) | Net Promoter Score among homeowners | > 50 | Whether homeowners would recommend FTW |
| Dispute rate | % of completed jobs with a formal dispute | < 3% | Whether the verification/vetting process is working |
| Review rate | % of completed jobs where the homeowner leaves a review | > 50% | Whether the feedback loop is healthy |

## When the Marketplace Is Working vs. When to Pivot

### Signs the Marketplace Is Working

- Contractors are logging in without prompting — they check FTW as part of their routine
- Homeowners are finding FTW through word of mouth (not just James's outreach)
- Time-to-first-bid is decreasing over time (supply is catching up to demand)
- At least 1 contractor has upgraded to Pro unprompted
- Fill rate is above 80% — almost every job post gets at least one bid
- Contractors are using the estimation tool for projects outside the marketplace (it's become their estimation tool, period)
- Repeat homeowner usage — someone posts a second project within 6 months

### Warning Signs (Adjust, Don't Panic)

- Fill rate below 50% — not enough contractor density. Recruit more supply before adding demand.
- Contractors sign up but ghost after Week 1 — the onboarding experience or tool value isn't clear enough. Fix UX.
- Homeowners post jobs but don't select any contractor — the bids may be too high, or the contractor profiles aren't trustworthy enough. Improve contractor profiles and review coverage.
- Estimation tool used but marketplace ignored — contractors like the tools but don't see the marketplace as valuable. This might be fine — white-label and SaaS tools revenue can sustain the business without marketplace volume.

### Red Flags (Consider Pivoting)

- After 90 days, fewer than 5 jobs have been matched through the platform despite active supply and demand
- Contractors openly say "I can find customers without this" and see no value in the marketplace
- Homeowners consistently say "I'd rather just ask a friend for a recommendation"
- CAC for both sides is above $100 and not decreasing
- Every match requires James to manually intervene (no organic activity)

### Pivot Options (If the Marketplace Fails)

1. **Pivot to pure SaaS tools.** Drop the marketplace. Sell estimation + project management as standalone software to contractors. White-label becomes the primary business. This is already revenue-generating via MHP.
2. **Pivot to lead-gen (reluctantly).** If subscription doesn't work, a per-lead model might. But this goes against FTW's core brand. Only consider if survival requires it.
3. **Pivot to B2B.** Sell ConstructionAI estimation directly to insurance companies, real estate firms, or property management companies who need quick cost estimates.
4. **Narrow the niche.** Instead of "all construction," become "the roofing marketplace for Mississippi." Extreme focus on one trade might achieve density that a broad marketplace cannot.

The key insight from every failed construction marketplace: they all died from lack of density, not lack of technology. FTW's technology is already built. The only question is whether Oxford can achieve sufficient density in a constrained geography to prove the model.
