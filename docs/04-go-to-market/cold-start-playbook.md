# Cold Start Playbook — Oxford, MS Launch

## How the Giants Solved It

**Uber:** Called limo companies with 10-12 cars. Paid them to have drivers respond to the app. Didn't need 10,000 cars day one — just enough to prove the model in one city. Key insight: they subsidized the supply side until organic demand could sustain it.

**Airbnb:** Scraped Craigslist for property owners. Sent personal pitches. First 30 days of in-person user engagement made the difference between success and failure. The founders personally photographed listings to improve quality — they did things that don't scale.

**Thumbtack:** Focused on high-demand, high-volume categories first (handyman, cleaning, moving). Built liquidity in specific categories before expanding. They learned that a marketplace with 10 great plumbers beats a marketplace with 1 of everything.

**DoorDash:** Started with one restaurant in Palo Alto. The founders personally delivered food. They didn't build the app until they'd validated demand by texting orders from a Google Voice number. First 6 months were entirely manual.

**Common thread:** Every successful marketplace launched in a single geography with a single category, subsidized one side of the market, and did things manually before automating. None of them launched "nationally" or tried to serve every category from day one.

## Week-by-Week Oxford Launch Timeline

### Pre-Launch (Weeks -4 to -1)

**Week -4: Foundation**
- File FTW LLC in Mississippi (same-day approval)
- Set up QuickBooks Online account for FTW
- Order business cards (simple: name, "FairTradeWorker.com", phone)
- Create FTW Google Business Profile for Oxford
- Set up Facebook Business Page
- Confirm platform is stable on mhpestimate.cloud — no crashes, estimation works

**Week -3: Hit List**
- Build a list of 25-30 contractors James knows or knows of in Oxford/Lafayette County
- Prioritize by trade: roofers (3-5), kitchen/bath remodelers (3-5), painters (3-5), general handyman (3-5)
- Look up each one: are they licensed with MSBOC? Do they have a website? Active on Facebook?
- Identify the 15 most likely to say yes — guys who are tech-curious, younger crews, or anyone who's complained about Angi/HomeAdvisor

**Week -2: First Calls**
- Call the top 15 contractors. See scripts below. Goal: 10 confirmed "I'll try it" commitments
- Schedule in-person coffee or jobsite visits with the 5 most enthusiastic
- Ask Josh (MHP) if he's willing to be referenced: "Josh is already using this, ask him about it"

**Week -1: Onboarding Prep**
- Walk Josh through the full contractor experience one more time — fix any friction
- Prepare a simple one-pager (PDF or printed) showing: what FTW does, what's free, what Pro costs
- Test the estimation tool with 3-4 real Oxford project addresses
- Make sure contractor signup flow works end-to-end without errors

### Launch Phase (Weeks 1-4)

**Week 1: Supply Activation**
- Personally onboard 5-8 contractors. Sit with them if needed — at their office, at a job site, at a coffee shop
- Walk each one through: create account, fill out profile, run a test estimate, see how their listing looks
- Do NOT promote to homeowners yet. Zero homeowner marketing
- Fix any bugs or UX issues that come up during real onboarding
- Goal: 5 contractors with complete profiles by end of week

**Week 2: Tool Adoption**
- Follow up with every onboarded contractor: "Have you tried running an estimate this week?"
- Onboard 3-5 more contractors (hit 8-12 total)
- Start posting in Oxford Facebook groups — but only as James, not as a company. "Hey, I built something for local contractors. Curious what y'all think."
- Get feedback: What do contractors like? What's confusing? What's missing?
- Goal: At least 5 contractors have used the estimation tool on a real project

**Week 3: Demand Seeding**
- If 10+ contractors are active: begin soft homeowner outreach
- Post in 3-4 Oxford Facebook community groups: "Looking for a contractor in Oxford? I built a free tool that connects you with licensed, verified local contractors."
- Ask 2-3 friendly contractors to share FTW with their existing customers
- Contact 3-5 real estate agents: "When your buyers ask 'know a good contractor?' — send them here"
- Goal: First 5-10 homeowner signups

**Week 4: First Match**
- Monitor for the first real homeowner-to-contractor connection through the platform
- When it happens, document everything: How did the homeowner find FTW? Which contractor responded? How long did it take?
- Follow up personally with both sides after the job starts
- If no match has happened, diagnose: Are homeowners posting jobs? Are contractors responding? Where's the bottleneck?
- Goal: At least 1 real job posted and matched through the platform

### Growth Phase (Weeks 5-12)

**Weeks 5-6:** Push for 3-5 active jobs flowing through the platform. Start collecting testimonials from satisfied homeowners.

**Weeks 7-8:** Launch NextDoor presence. Begin content marketing (project cost articles for Oxford). Ask best contractors to upgrade to Pro tier.

**Weeks 9-10:** First paid ad experiment — $200 budget on Facebook, targeting Oxford homeowners 30-55 who own homes. Measure cost per homeowner signup.

**Weeks 11-12:** Evaluate cold start metrics (see below). Decide whether to double down on Oxford or adjust strategy before expanding.

## Contractor Recruitment Scripts

### Script 1: Warm Call (Someone James Knows)

> "Hey [name], it's James. Listen, I've been building something I think you'd actually want to use. It's called FairTradeWorker — it's a free platform where you can run AI estimates, manage your projects, and get connected with local homeowners looking for work. No lead fees, no per-job charges. Josh Harris at MHP is already using it. Can I buy you a coffee this week and show you? Takes 10 minutes."

### Script 2: Warm-ish Call (Someone James Knows Of)

> "Hey [name], this is James Walton. I'm a contractor here in Oxford and I built a tool for guys like us. It's called FairTradeWorker — free estimation tools, project management, and it connects you with homeowners in the area. No Angi-style lead fees. A few contractors here are already on it. I'd love to show you — can I swing by your shop or meet you at a job site this week?"

### Script 3: Handling the "What's the Catch?" Objection

> "No catch. The basic tools are free forever — estimation, invoicing, profile. We make money from an optional Pro subscription ($49/month) that gives you priority placement and extra features. But you don't have to go Pro to use it. I built this because I was tired of platforms that nickel-and-dime contractors. Try it free, and if you don't like it, no hard feelings."

### Script 4: The Competitive Angle

> "I'll be straight with you — [competitor name] is already on the platform. If a homeowner in Oxford searches for a [trade], they're going to see [competitor] but not you. I want to make sure you're represented. It takes 5 minutes to set up a profile."

### Script 5: Real Estate Agent Partnership Pitch

> "Hey [agent name], this is James Walton with FairTradeWorker. I know your buyers constantly ask you for contractor recommendations. We built a platform with licensed, background-checked contractors here in Oxford. Instead of keeping a mental list, you can just send them to FairTradeWorker.com. It's free for you — and it makes you look good because every contractor on there is verified. Can I send you a link to check it out?"

## The First 30 Days for a New Contractor on FTW

**Day 1: Signup and Profile**
- Create account (email + password, or Google login)
- Fill out profile: company name, trades, service area, years of experience, photos
- FCRA disclosure + authorization for background check (if applicable)
- Upload license and insurance documents

**Days 2-7: Tool Discovery**
- Run first AI estimate using ConstructionAI
- Explore the dashboard: see job categories, check how their profile looks to homeowners
- Receive a personal follow-up from James: "How's everything looking? Any questions?"

**Days 8-14: Habituation**
- Receive push notification or email when a homeowner posts a job in their trade/area
- Submit first bid on a homeowner job
- Use estimation tool on a real project they're quoting outside the platform
- Get comfortable with the interface

**Days 15-21: Engagement**
- Check the platform 2-3 times per week
- Respond to at least one job inquiry
- Optionally: share their FTW profile link with an existing customer for a review

**Days 22-30: Conversion Decision**
- James follows up: "Has FTW been useful? Any feedback?"
- Contractor decides whether to stay on Free or upgrade to Pro
- If upgrading, Pro benefits kick in: priority placement, advanced estimation, enhanced profile

## Leading Indicators: How to Know If Cold Start Is Working

Don't wait for revenue to judge success. Track these weekly:

| Metric | Target by Week 4 | Target by Week 8 | Target by Week 12 |
|--------|-------------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| Contractors with complete profiles | 10 | 15 | 20 |
| Contractors who logged in this week | 7 | 10 | 15 |
| Estimates run through ConstructionAI | 20 | 50 | 100 |
| Homeowner signups | 5 | 20 | 50 |
| Jobs posted by homeowners | 1 | 5 | 15 |
| Bids submitted by contractors | 1 | 8 | 25 |
| Matches (homeowner picks a contractor) | 0 | 2 | 8 |
| Time from job post to first bid | n/a | < 48 hours | < 24 hours |
| Contractor weekly return rate | 50% | 60% | 65% |

**Green signals (cold start is working):**
- Contractors are logging in without James prompting them
- Homeowners are posting jobs organically (not just from James's Facebook posts)
- Time-to-first-bid is decreasing week over week
- At least 1 contractor has asked about Pro unprompted
- Word of mouth: "My neighbor told me about this"

**Red signals (cold start is stalling):**
- Contractors sign up but never log in again after Day 1
- Homeowners sign up but don't post jobs
- Jobs get posted but no contractor bids within 72 hours
- James is the only source of new signups on both sides

## Plan B: What to Do If Supply-Side Recruitment Stalls

If you can't get 10 contractors on the platform in the first 3 weeks:

**Tactic 1: Sweeten the Deal**
- Offer first 6 months of Pro free to the first 10 contractors who join
- "Founding member" badge on their profile — permanent, even if they cancel Pro later
- This costs nothing in real dollars and creates urgency

**Tactic 2: Go to Where They Are**
- Show up at the local lumber yard (84 Lumber, local hardware stores) at 6:30 AM
- Attend the next Lafayette County Home Builders Association meeting
- Post a flyer at the MSBOC office (contractors go there for license renewals)

**Tactic 3: Leverage Josh Harder**
- Ask Josh to personally text 5 contractors he knows: "Hey, I've been using this new platform and it's actually good. You should check it out."
- A recommendation from a peer contractor is 10x more powerful than a pitch from a platform founder

**Tactic 4: Expand the Geography Slightly**
- If Oxford proper doesn't have enough density, extend to Water Valley, Batesville, and Pontotoc — still within 30-minute drive
- Don't go to Memphis or Jackson yet — that's a different market

**Tactic 5: Pivot to a Single Trade**
- If you can't get 10 contractors across all trades, get 5 great roofers
- Launch as "the roofing platform for Oxford" — become the best at one thing
- Expand to other trades once roofing is proven

**Tactic 6: Manual Matching (Concierge Mode)**
- If the platform isn't getting organic matches, James manually matches homeowners with contractors via phone/text
- This is what DoorDash did — the founders delivered food themselves
- It doesn't scale, but it proves the value proposition

## Cold-Start Approaches: What Worked vs What Failed

| Approach | Example | Worked? | Why |
|----------|---------|---------|-----|
| Personal recruitment by founder | Uber, Airbnb, FTW plan | Yes | Founders understand the user, can adapt the pitch in real-time, build trust |
| Paying supply side to join | Uber (guaranteed fares) | Yes, short-term | Gets initial supply, but must transition to organic or bleed money |
| Scraping existing platforms | Airbnb (Craigslist) | Yes | Users already proven to be interested in the category |
| Launching nationally with ads | Homejoy, many failed startups | No | Too thin — no density in any market, poor experience everywhere |
| Relying on SEO alone for launch | Most failed marketplaces | No | SEO takes 6+ months; cold start needs users in weeks |
| Building both sides simultaneously | Common VC-funded approach | Rarely | Splits focus; better to nail supply side first |
| Free tools as supply-side wedge | HubSpot (CRM), FTW plan | Yes | Users join for free value, marketplace is the upsell |
| Constrained geography | DoorDash (Palo Alto), FTW plan | Yes | Creates density, proves model, then replicates |

## Key Principle

"Get the supply side first — if you get the right suppliers, the customers will experience their high quality service and then do the marketing for you." (Andrew Chen / Lenny Rachitsky)

The Oxford cold start isn't about growing fast. It's about proving that 10 contractors and 20 homeowners can transact through FTW better than they can through word of mouth alone. If that works, everything else is replication.
